## The Problem

Graph neural networks promise relational reasoning, but deeper stacks often lose discrimination as node representations drift toward each other. That oversmoothing behavior is widely discussed and often poorly diagnosed. I wanted to study where it showed up, how to measure it, and which mitigations actually changed the outcome.

The goal was not to produce a new architecture. It was to understand the failure mode precisely enough to make architectural decisions with evidence.

## Approach

I paired classification metrics with internal representation metrics. Task accuracy can remain stable for a while even when the embedding space is already collapsing, so I tracked pairwise similarity and class separability layer by layer. That made the degradation visible before it became obvious at the output.

Residual paths and normalization helped, but only up to a point. The deeper lesson was that depth should be justified by the graph signal itself, not imported from image-model habits.

## Results

The experiments showed a fairly consistent depth zone where oversmoothing started to dominate on the selected graphs. More importantly, the project produced a workflow for diagnosing the problem instead of guessing at it.

## Key Learnings

- Internal representation metrics often reveal failure earlier than top-line accuracy.
- Deeper graph models need graph-specific justification, not generic depth bias.
- Residual structure helps, but it does not excuse poor evaluation discipline.
